Based on authoritative IRS sources, here are the key rules for home office deductions for self-employed individuals:

## Qualification Requirements

There must be exclusive use of a portion of the home for conducting business on a regular basis. The home must generally be the taxpayer's principal place of business. However, a taxpayer can meet this requirement if administrative or management activities are conducted at the home and there is no other fixed location to perform these duties.

Employees are not eligible to claim the home office deduction.

## Two Calculation Methods

**Simplified Method:**
Taxpayers may choose a simplified option with a rate of $5 a square foot for business use of the home, with a maximum size of 300 square feet and a maximum deduction of $1,500.

**Regular Method:**
You compute the business use of home deduction by dividing expenses of operating the home between personal and business use, deducting direct business expenses in full and allocating indirect total expenses of the home to the percentage of the home floor space used for business.

## Reporting and Deductible Expenses

Self-employed taxpayers filing Schedule C (Form 1040), Profit or Loss From Business (Sole Proprietorship) first compute this deduction on Form 8829, Expenses for Business Use of Your Home.

Deductible expenses may include mortgage interest, insurance, utilities, repairs, maintenance, depreciation and rent.

For comprehensive guidance, consult Publication 587, Business Use of Your Home (Including Use by Daycare Providers) at https://www.irs.gov/publications/p587

Here are the **IRS rules for the home office deduction** specifically for **self‑employed individuals**, based strictly on authoritative IRS sources:

---

## 1. Qualifications for Deduction  
To qualify, a portion of your home must be used **exclusively and regularly** for business, and it must meet one of the following criteria:  
- It serves as your **principal place of business**, where administrative or managerial activities are conducted and there is no other fixed location for such activities.  
- It is a space where you **meet clients, customers, or patients** in the normal course of business.  
- It is a **separate structure not attached** to your home, used exclusively and regularly for business.  
- It is used regularly to **store inventory or product samples**, and the home is the sole fixed location for that business.  
- It serves as a **daycare facility** under certain conditions.  
([irs.gov](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc509?utm_source=openai))

---

## 2. Methods for Calculating the Deduction  
You may choose between two methods:

### A. Regular (Standard) Method  
- You calculate allowable expenses by dividing home-related expenses between business and personal use.  
- **Direct expenses** (specific to the business part) are fully deductible.  
- **Indirect expenses** (e.g., utilities, mortgage interest, insurance) are allocated based on the percentage of your home used for business.  
- Self-employed individuals must use **Form 8829** to figure this deduction for Schedule C (Form 1040).  
- The deduction is **subject to a gross income limitation**: it cannot exceed business income derived from the home, after subtracting other business expenses. In years where expenses exceed this limit, you may **carry over the excess** to future years (if using the regular method).  
([irs.gov](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc509?utm_source=openai))

### B. Simplified (Safe Harbor) Method  
- You deduct **$5 per square foot** of home used for business, limited to **300 square feet** (maximum deduction of $1,500).  
- No need to calculate actual expenses or depreciation.  
- Depreciation is treated as **zero**, so **no basis reduction or recapture** is required if you later sell the home.  
- You claim the deduction directly on **Schedule C, Line 30**, indicating election of this method.  
- Other allowable home-related expenses (e.g., mortgage interest, real estate taxes, casualty losses) may still be claimed in full on **Schedule A** — not apportioned.  
- The simplified method is also subject to the **gross income limitation**, and **no carryover** of disallowed amounts is permitted.  
([irs.gov](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/simplified-option-for-home-office-deduction?utm_source=openai))

---

## 3. Special Rules & Considerations  
- **Election timing**: You select the method by using it on your timely filed, original tax return. You cannot change methods later for the same year.  
([irs.gov](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/simplified-option-for-home-office-deduction?utm_source=openai))

- **Switching methods**: If you use the simplified method one year and revert to the regular method in a later year, you must still calculate depreciation using IRS’s optional depreciation tables based on the **remaining business basis**.  
([irs.gov](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/simplified-option-for-home-office-deduction?utm_source=openai))

- **Depreciation recapture**: Under the regular method, depreciation must be recaptured when you sell the home – you must reduce your basis by the greater of allowed or allowable depreciation. This does **not** apply to years where the simplified method was used.  
([irs.gov](https://www.irs.gov/faqs/sale-or-trade-of-business-depreciation-rentals/depreciation-recapture/depreciation-recapture-3?utm_source=openai))

- **Carryover rules**: Only the regular method allows **carryover** of unallowed expenses to future years. The simplified method does **not**.  
([irs.gov](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/simplified-option-for-home-office-deduction?utm_source=openai))

- For **farmers** (Schedule F filers) or **partners**, specific worksheets in **Publication 587** apply instead of Form 8829.  
([irs.gov](https://www.irs.gov/taxtopics/tc509?utm_source=openai))

---

## 4. Claiming and Recordkeeping  
- Use **Form 8829** for the regular method; report the deduction on **Schedule C, Line 30**.  
- Use the **simplified method** by entering square footage details directly on **Schedule C, Line 30**—no Form 8829 required.  
- Maintain records that support your deduction:
  - For the regular method: proof of expenses, basis, depreciation records, square footage.
  - For either method: documentation of exclusive and regular business use.  
([irs.gov](https://www.irs.gov/instructions/i8829?utm_source=openai))

To qualify for the home office deduction, self-employed individuals must meet specific IRS requirements regarding the use of a portion of their home for business. Employees are not eligible to claim the home office deduction.

Generally, to deduct expenses for the business use of a home, the taxpayer must use part of their home:
*   **Exclusively and Regularly** as their principal place of business.
*   **Exclusively and Regularly** as a place where they meet or deal with patients, clients, or customers in the normal course of their trade or business.
*   In the case of a **separate structure** not attached to the home, in connection with their trade or business.
*   On a regular basis for certain **storage use** of inventory or product samples.
*   As a **daycare facility**.

**Key Requirements:**

1.  **Exclusive Use:** A specific area of the home must be used *only* for trade or business purposes. It can be a room or a separately identifiable space, but it cannot be used for any personal purposes. Exceptions to the exclusive use rule apply for certain storage of inventory or product samples, and for daycare facilities.
2.  **Regular Use:** The business part of the home must be used for business on a regular basis. Incidental or occasional business use does not qualify.
3.  **Principal Place of Business:** The home office must be the taxpayer's principal place of business, or a place where administrative or management activities are conducted exclusively and regularly, and there is no other fixed location where substantial administrative or management activities of the business are conducted.

**Methods for Calculating the Deduction:**

Self-employed individuals can choose between two methods to calculate their home office deduction:

1.  **Regular Method (Actual Expenses):** This method involves calculating the actual expenses related to the business use of the home. Deductible expenses can include a percentage of direct expenses (e.g., painting and repairs in the business area) and indirect expenses (e.g., mortgage interest, rent, insurance, utilities, maintenance, general repairs, and depreciation). The percentage is generally determined by the portion of the home devoted to business use, often based on square footage. Taxpayers using this method typically use Form 8829, Expenses for Business Use of Your Home, with Schedule C (Form 1040), Profit or Loss From Business.
2.  **Simplified Method:** This is an alternative to calculating actual expenses, offering a standard deduction. Taxpayers can deduct $5 per square foot for the business use of their home, up to a maximum of 300 square feet, resulting in a maximum deduction of $1,500 per year. With this method, depreciation for the business portion of the home cannot be claimed, but qualified itemized deductions for mortgage interest, real property taxes, and casualty losses for the home can still be claimed in full without allocation.

More detailed information can be found in IRS Publication 587, Business Use of Your Home (Including Use by Daycare Providers).

## Overview

26 CFR 1.199A-1 provides operational rules for calculating the section 199A(a) qualified business income deduction under section 199A of the Internal Revenue Code.

## Definition of QBI

Qualified business income (QBI) means, for any taxable year, the net amount of qualified items of income, gain, deduction, and loss with respect to any trade or business of the taxpayer, provided the other requirements of this section and section 199A are satisfied.

## Items Excluded from QBI

The following items are not taken into account as qualified items of income, gain, deduction, or loss and thus are not included in determining QBI: any item of short-term capital gain, short-term capital loss, long-term capital gain, or long-term capital loss; any dividend or income equivalent to a dividend; and any interest income other than interest income which is properly allocable to a trade or business.

The Code of Federal Regulations (CFR), specifically Title 26 (Internal Revenue), addresses the Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction primarily under **26 CFR Part 1, Sections 1.199A-1 through 1.199A-6**. These regulations provide detailed guidance on the application of Internal Revenue Code (IRC) Section 199A, which was enacted by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017.

Here's a summary of what 26 CFR says about the QBI deduction:

**General Principles:**
*   The QBI deduction allows certain individuals, partnerships, S corporations, trusts, and estates to deduct up to 20% of their qualified business income from a domestic business, plus up to 20% of their combined qualified Real Estate Investment Trust (REIT) dividends and qualified Publicly Traded Partnership (PTP) income.
*   The deduction is generally limited to the lesser of the combined QBI amount or 20% of the taxpayer's taxable income exceeding net capital gain.
*   The regulations clarify that the QBI deduction does not affect the adjusted basis of a partner's interest in a partnership, a shareholder's stock in an S corporation, or an S corporation's accumulated adjustments account.

**Key Definitions and Rules:**
*   **Qualified Business Income (QBI):** Defined as the net amount of qualified items of income, gain, deduction, and loss with respect to any qualified trade or business of the taxpayer. It does not include qualified REIT dividends or qualified PTP income.
*   **Qualified Trade or Business:** The regulations define what constitutes a qualified trade or business and address limitations, notably concerning specified service trades or businesses (SSTBs). If a trade or business is an SSTB, no QBI, W-2 wages, or unadjusted basis immediately after acquisition (UBIA) of qualified property from the SSTB may be taken into account by individuals whose taxable income exceeds a certain phase-in range.
*   **W-2 Wages:** The regulations provide rules for determining W-2 wages, which are used in limiting the deduction for taxpayers with taxable income above certain thresholds.
*   **Unadjusted Basis Immediately After Acquisition (UBIA) of Qualified Property:** This refers to the unadjusted basis of tangible depreciable property held by the qualified trade or business. Like W-2 wages, UBIA of qualified property is used in the deduction limitation calculation.
*   **Phase-in Range:** This is a range of taxable income where the limitations based on W-2 wages and UBIA of qualified property (and the SSTB limitation) gradually apply.
*   **Aggregation Rules:** 26 CFR § 1.199A-4 outlines rules for taxpayers and passthrough entities that choose to aggregate two or more trades or businesses for purposes of the QBI deduction.
*   **Pass-through Entities:** For partnerships and S corporations, section 199A is applied at the partner or shareholder level. The regulations provide rules for how these entities report QBI, W-2 wages, and UBIA of qualified property to their owners or beneficiaries.
*   **Suspended Losses:** The regulations address the treatment of previously suspended losses when determining QBI.
*   **Special Rules for Cooperatives:** Section 199A(g) provides a special entity-level deduction for specified agricultural or horticultural cooperatives, similar to the domestic production activities deduction under former section 199.

**Regulatory History:**
The regulations under 26 CFR Part 1, Section 199A have been updated since the initial enactment of IRC Section 199A. For instance, final regulations were published in the Federal Register in February 2019 and amended in June 2020 to provide further guidance.
